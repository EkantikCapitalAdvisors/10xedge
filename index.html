01 · The Reframe

Floor before return.

Most managers tell you their return. Almost none tell you their floor. We lead with the floor, because the floor is the only thing you actually control before the edge takes over. A hard daily loss limit is concrete and visceral in a way “fixed-fractional R sizing” never is.

02 · The Mechanic

The three jobs of the daily floor.

1

A risk budget

Each session you risk at most the day’s floor. The engine allocates that budget across the day’s trades.

2

A circuit breaker

Hit the floor and the session ends — no revenge trades, no spiral. Where real edges get killed by their operators.

3

A falsifiability floor

A pre-committed, observable, binary kill condition for the day — the daily-granularity edge of a witnessed protocol.

04 · The Architecture

The floor is the architecture.

Active capital is the smallest tier; the engine carries no separate reserve. The floor does the job the buffer does elsewhere — floor-defense, not buffer-defense. Each full-stop loss risks $500 per ES (1R), and the daily loss limit caps the day at $600 (see §09). As the account grows, risk-per-stop rises in steps with the ES count; as it falls, size de-scales with it.

Floor-defense vs buffer-defense are two architectures. This page is the floor one — illustrative, not a forecast.

09 · Risk Management & Falsification

The governance architecture.

Four layers sit beneath the floor: a risk-management base, a sit-out ladder that throttles trading after losses, and two falsifiability tracks — Operator (am I following my own process?) and Edge (is the edge still working?). All are committed to in writing, in advance, and witness-countersigned by Manish Dharod — not discretionary.
